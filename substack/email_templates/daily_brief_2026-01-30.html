The Daily Macro & Market Brief

January 30, 2026

Friday

The S&P 500 breached 7,000 for the first time as technology earnings revealed a stark divergence in how investors view AI spending. Meta surged 8-10% after committing up to $135 billion in 2026 capex—an 87% year-over-year increase—while Microsoft plunged 6-10% despite record spending as Azure cloud growth of 38% failed to keep pace with capex increases of 66%.

Tech Earnings Create Winners and Losers

Apple reported record Q1 revenue of $144 billion, up 16%, with iPhone sales reaching $85 billion and China revenue hitting $25 billion for the first time in four years. However, gains faded as the company's AI strategy remains unclear despite a Google/Gemini partnership. Tesla doubled capex guidance to $20 billion—twice estimates—for AI, robotics, and potential in-house semiconductor fabrication.

The software sector faced a broader rout. SAP crashed 13-15% on cloud weakness and missed order backlogs, while ServiceNow dropped 7-8% amid fears that AI startups are disrupting traditional software incumbents. The divergence reflects markets shifting from a "rising tide lifts all boats" AI narrative to picking specific winners and losers.

Single-stock volatility hit historic highs as intra-sector correlation in big tech fell to 20-25% from previous ranges of 40-50%. Microsoft alone wiped 50 points off the S&P 500, offset by Meta's gains.

Memory Supply Constraints Drive Parabolic Moves

Memory chip shortages drove explosive moves across storage companies. SanDisk and Western Digital posted record gross margins of 51% versus 42% estimates, while Seagate jumped 20%. The shortage stems from a 300,000-800,000 ton copper supply deficit in a 26 million ton market, with no near-term substitution available. New fab capacity won't come online for 2+ years, creating sustained pricing power but input cost pressures for Apple, Tesla, and other device makers.

Samsung and SK Hynix are boosting capex for high-bandwidth memory production as supply constraints represent the "#1 bottleneck in AI" according to industry analysts. The memory shortage is contributing to a broader shift favoring semiconductor capital equipment companies like Lam Research and KLA, which benefit from all AI spending scenarios regardless of which companies ultimately win.

Fed Holds with Two Dovish Dissents

The Federal Reserve held rates at 4.25-4.50% with two dissenting votes from Christopher Waller and Adriana Kugler, who favored a 25 basis point cut. The dissents were notable given that typically dovish governor Michelle Bowman sided with the majority, suggesting underlying hawkishness despite the cutting votes.

Markets show no urgency for near-term easing, pricing only a 14% probability of cuts in March or April—Powell's final meetings before his term ends May 23. The first cut isn't fully priced until July, with only two cuts expected for all of 2026.

Fed Chair Speculation Intensifies

President Trump indicated he would announce his Fed chair nominee "next week," with Kevin Warsh emerging as the leading candidate. Markets reacted hawkishly to Warsh speculation, with the dollar gaining 0.5% and 30-year yields rising 5 basis points in the steepest curve move of the Asian session. Equity futures fell 1% as long-duration tech stocks were pressured by the steeper yield curve.

Warsh, a former Fed governor from 2006-2011, is viewed as more hawkish than alternatives like BlackRock's Rick Rieder despite recent alignment with Trump's rate cut preferences. His previous Fed tenure showed inflation intolerance, and he has criticized the institution as needing "regime change."

Commodities Hit Records on Multiple Drivers

Gold breached $5,500 and silver topped $120 per ounce on sustained dollar weakness and what traders call the "debasement trade"—investors diversifying from overweight dollar exposure into real assets. Silver is up approximately 60% year-to-date in an extreme rally that has proven costly for those attempting to short the top.

Copper hit fresh record highs above $13,942 per ton, driven by the supply deficit and AI infrastructure demand. Oil rallied for the third consecutive day, with Brent crude reaching $69.73 as Trump threatened military strikes against Iran "with speed and violence" if no nuclear deal is reached.

Dollar Weakness Persists Despite Intervention

The dollar fell to four-year lows despite Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent's "strong dollar" rhetoric, which directly contradicted Trump's demands for rate cuts. European investors are now actively discussing hedging their U.S. equity exposure—a conversation that would have been unthinkable previously. The euro struggled above 1.20 against the dollar.

Economic Data and Risks

Jobless claims came in at 209,000, a slight uptick but below the prior week, as the Fed cited a "stabilizing" labor market despite recent layoff announcements. Consumer confidence showed jobs plentiful sentiment falling to 23.9% from 28%—the lowest since 2014 despite strong GDP growth tracking 3-4% for Q4. Government shutdown risk loomed with a midnight January 31 deadline on Homeland Security funding as Democrats demanded immigration enforcement restrictions including body cameras and warrant requirements.

OpenAI Funding and Burn Rate Concerns

OpenAI is raising $40 billion from Nvidia ($20 billion), Amazon ($10 billion), and Microsoft (several billion more) amid reports of $9 billion monthly cash burn. The funding comes as the company faces existential pressure as the only frontier AI model without its own distribution channel, forcing it into advertising—previously seen as a "desperate" signal.

The developments highlight growing AI ROI uncertainty that has left Nvidia dead money for six months and Oracle struggling despite the sector's massive capital commitments. Amazon is separately in talks for a $50 billion investment in a round valuing OpenAI at $830 billion, underscoring both the company's promise and cash needs.
