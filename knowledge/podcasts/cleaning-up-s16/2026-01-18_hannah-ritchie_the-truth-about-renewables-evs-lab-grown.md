# The Truth About Renewables, EVs & Lab-Grown Meat: Hannah Ritchie on Clearing the Air

## 1. Episode Overview

**Guest Background:** Hannah Ritchie is deputy editor and lead on science outreach for Our World in Data, and a researcher at the Oxford Martin School of Global Development. This is her second appearance on Cleaning Up, following her 2023 debut discussing her first book "It's Not the End of the World."

**Main Topic:** Ritchie's new book "Clearing the Air" addresses 50 common misconceptions about climate solutions, from renewable energy and electric vehicles to nuclear power and meat substitutes. The conversation explores how misinformation—from both opponents and proponents of climate action—hampers progress.

**Key Thesis:** We have solid, data-driven answers to most questions about climate solutions, but public confusion persists due to conflicting information from multiple sources. The book aims to equip people with factual grounding so society can "stop going round and round in circles" and actually take action.

## 2. Major Themes Discussed

### Electric Vehicles: Debunking Persistent Myths

**The Climate Impact Question:** Ritchie addresses the common claim that EVs are "just as bad" as internal combustion vehicles due to battery manufacturing emissions.

**Key Insight:** While EV manufacturing does have a slightly higher carbon footprint initially (primarily from battery production), **drivers pay off this "carbon debt" in 1-2 years** of typical UK driving. After that point, the EV's per-mile emissions are dramatically lower. Even in coal-heavy grids like China's, EVs still produce fewer emissions per mile than petrol cars—and this advantage only improves as electricity grids decarbonize.

Ritchie emphasizes: "This is as bad as it's ever going to be when it comes to the carbon footprint of electric cars. It will only get better as we decarbonize."

**Air Pollution Concerns:** On local air pollution, EVs eliminate tailpipe emissions entirely—the largest contributor to urban air pollution. While questions exist about tire wear (EVs are heavier) and road dust, **EVs win decisively on brake wear** (due to regenerative braking) and especially on tailpipe emissions, more than offsetting any other concerns.

**Cold Weather Performance:** EVs do experience range reduction in cold weather—about 15% on average in Norwegian studies. However, Ritchie stresses this is **manageable for most drivers** and that internal combustion vehicles also lose efficiency in cold conditions. The key difference is transparency: manufacturers should clearly communicate these limitations to consumers.

**Battery Degradation:** Unlike phone batteries that degrade rapidly, **EV batteries retain 85%+ capacity even after 200,000 miles**. The industry dynamics differ fundamentally: phone manufacturers benefit from degradation driving replacement purchases, while car manufacturers know battery longevity is essential for market acceptance.

**Supporting Evidence:** Ritchie cites the dramatic progress from the 2011 Nissan Leaf to 2026 models: range increased 4x (75 to 300+ miles), power doubled, charging speed tripled, and **cost remained roughly the same in nominal terms**—meaning real costs dropped by two-thirds.

### Renewable Energy: Addressing the Variability Challenge

**The Intermittency Question:** "What happens when the wind doesn't blow and the sun doesn't shine?" is perhaps the most common objection to renewables.

**Ritchie's Framework:** She breaks down solutions by timescale:
- **Minutes to hours:** Batteries (increasingly cost-competitive)
- **Hours to days:** Multiple solutions including batteries, emerging technologies like iron redox batteries, heat storage, and **balancing with other low-carbon sources** (nuclear, geothermal)
- **Longer periods:** Long-range transmission (it's always sunny or windy somewhere) and potentially nuclear for baseload

**Critical Insight:** Ritchie argues that **"so much of the conversation focuses on what we'll do for the last 5% rather than getting up to 90-95%."** In many parts of the world, solar, wind, and batteries alone can achieve very high penetration rates relatively cheaply. Even reaching 95% clean power globally would be "extremely, extremely happy" territory.

She notes: "Even if that 5% is just gas peakers, that's actually better than doing nothing because we're worried about having 5% gas peakers."

**Host's Technical Addition:** Liebreich clarifies that spinning reserves (backup gas plants) don't consume fuel at the same rate as active generation. They provide grid stability services while using minimal fuel, making the "you'll burn gas anyway" argument completely false.

### Grid Infrastructure: The Real Bottleneck

**The Question:** Can we build electricity grids fast enough to support renewable deployment?

**Ritchie's Assessment:** This is actually **a bigger barrier than intermittency**. Grid connection queues in the US and UK contain enormous amounts of potential capacity—sometimes comparable to total existing grid capacity. The limiting factor isn't technology but permitting and connection processes.

**Solutions Discussed:**
- **Merit-based queuing:** The UK is switching from "first-come, first-served" to prioritizing projects most ready for construction
- **Permitting reform:** Current systems were designed for a few large fossil fuel plants, not numerous smaller renewable installations
- **Learning from the Global South:** Developing countries are deploying renewables faster partly because they're accustomed to electricity demand growth and grid expansion

**Key Contrast:** Developed countries had flat or declining electricity demand for decades. Now facing growth from electrification, data centers, and EVs, they're struggling to adapt. As Liebreich notes, "there's enormous grinding of gears because they're not able to deal with" demand growth after 20 years of stagnation.

### Cost Competitiveness of Renewables

**The Question:** Aren't renewables too expensive?

**Ritchie's Answer:** Using the Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE)—which she acknowledges has limitations—**solar and wind are now cheaper than fossil fuels in almost every country**. This represents a dramatic shift from just 10 years ago.

**Important Nuance:** Increasingly, **solar plus batteries or wind plus batteries are also cheaper than fossil fuels** in many regions, including much of Europe and India. This gets you to very high penetration rates (90-95%) relatively cheaply.

**The Final 5% Problem:** Costs can become "astronomical" for the last few percentage points of decarbonization. However, Ritchie questions whether spending enormous sums on that final 5% makes sense when the same money could decarbonize transport or heating more cost-effectively.

### Nuclear Power: Expensive but Potentially Useful

**The Cost Reality:** Ritchie acknowledges that **nuclear is more expensive than renewables** on an LCOE basis, and also more expensive than fossil fuels (before accounting for climate damages).

**Geographic Variation:** Construction costs vary dramatically—US and UK nuclear projects are "eyewateringly expensive," while China and South Korea build at a fraction of the cost. However, even in Asia, nuclear remains more expensive than solar and wind.

**Potential Role:** Nuclear might be cost-competitive for addressing that expensive "final 10%" of grid decarbonization, where alternatives like hydrogen could be even more costly. Ritchie suggests: "For the bulk of getting your electricity demand, I would say nuclear is expensive. It could play a role in that final component."

**Construction Time:** Historically, most nuclear plants took 8-10 years to build, with China and South Korea achieving 5-6 years. Western countries previously built at similar speeds but have dramatically slowed. Ritchie is "relatively pessimistic" about Western countries speeding up construction.

**Host's Counterpoint:** Liebreich challenges the comparison with Asian nuclear costs, arguing that countries like China can hide costs through free land, subsidized capital, military-industrial synergies, and lack of decommissioning funds—advantages unavailable in transparent Western markets. He also notes that **nuclear's inflexibility makes it unsuitable as backup power**, as it must run continuously to be economical.

### Mineral Dependencies: A Different Kind of Risk

**The Concern:** Won't clean energy create dangerous dependencies on a few countries (primarily China) for critical minerals, similar to fossil fuel dependencies?

**Ritchie's Distinction:** She draws a crucial difference between the two dependencies:

**Fossil Fuel Dependency:** "When you rely on a country for fossil fuels, you are basically tied into them forever. You need a continuous supply of fuel year after year, month after month, day after day in order to get power."

**Mineral Dependency:** "Once you've built the stuff or bought the stuff, you're no longer beholden to whoever supplied you with those minerals or those goods. Once you have a solar panel, the fuel is free."

**Key Argument:** Mineral dependency creates risks for the **speed of the energy transition** (if supply is disrupted, you can't build more capacity), but not the same immediate energy security risk as fossil fuel cutoffs.

**Host's Rebuttal:** Liebreich offers a more nuanced view, arguing that **supply chain dependency creates slower but equally crippling vulnerabilities**. Without access to replacement parts and new equipment, systems degrade over time. He also raises concerns about data collection through Chinese-made cars and solar inverters, suggesting security risks beyond simple energy supply.

**Ritchie's Response:** She clarifies she's not advocating zero concern or high dependency, but rather that **"this dependency is not a reason for us not to proceed. The point is that we need to do the other stuff at the same time"**—meaning diversification and domestic capacity building should happen alongside deployment.

**Trade-offs:** Ritchie acknowledges that diversifying away from China involves accepting higher costs and longer timelines. Countries like India, Vietnam, Thailand, and African nations could become alternative suppliers, but this will take "a decade or two."

### Meat Substitutes: Barriers Beyond Logic

**The Climate Connection:** Meat substitutes appear in a climate book because **producing protein from plants uses far less land and generates much lower emissions** than animal agriculture.

**Cost Barrier:** Most meat substitutes are currently **more expensive than cheaper meat options**, creating an immediate obstacle despite potential environmental benefits.

**Trust and Perception Issues:** 
- **Ultra-processed concerns:** Growing narrative about health risks of ultra-processed foods has "very much wrapped up" meat substitutes
- **Taste perceptions:** Interestingly, **blind taste tests often favor plant-based products**, suggesting the aversion is psychological rather than gustatory
- **Familiarity:** People stick with what they know

**Naming Controversy:** The conversation touches on whether to call lab-grown meat simply "meat." Liebreich argues for complete transparency, noting that **"meat everybody knows comes from animals not from animal cells grown in factories."** Ritchie agrees that differentiation is important, suggesting terms like "plant-based burger" rather than claiming generic terms like "burger" or "sausage" exclusively for animal products.

## 3. Frameworks & Mental Models

### The Timescale Framework for Grid Flexibility

Ritchie presents a useful framework for thinking about renewable intermittency by **breaking solutions into timescales**:
- Short-term (minutes-hours): Batteries
- Medium-term (hours-days): Diverse storage, other low-carbon sources
- Long-term (days-weeks): Transmission, potentially nuclear baseload

**Application:** This prevents conflating different challenges and helps identify appropriate solutions for each timescale rather than seeking one-size-fits-all answers.

### The 90-95% Rule

A recurring theme is that **getting to 90-95% of a solution is relatively cheap and straightforward, while the final 5-10% becomes exponentially expensive**. This applies to:
- Grid decarbonization
- EV adoption
- Any systemic transition

**Application:** Focus resources on rapid deployment to high penetration rather than obsessing over perfect solutions. The final percentage points might be better addressed by moving to adjacent problems (e.g., decarbonizing transport instead of pursuing 100% clean electricity).

### Dependency Type Distinction

Ritchie's framework distinguishes between:
- **Continuous dependency** (fossil fuels): Requires ongoing supply to function
- **Capital dependency** (clean energy equipment): One-time purchase, then independent operation

**Application:** This helps assess true energy security risks versus transition risks. However, as Liebreich notes, this framework may underweight supply chain vulnerabilities for maintenance and replacement.

### The Spectrum of Persuadability

Ritchie explicitly describes her audience strategy as targeting three groups:
- **Already convinced:** Won't change minds, but can be equipped with better information
- **Strongly opposed:** Unlikely to persuade, not the target
- **Middle ground:** Confused by conflicting information, willing to engage—the primary audience

**Application:** Don't waste energy trying to convince the unconvinceable. Instead, **"inoculate" the middle ground** against misinformation by providing clear, data-driven answers.

## 4. Contrarian Takes

### Misinformation Comes From Everywhere

**Contrarian View:** Ritchie challenges the assumption that misinformation primarily comes from fossil fuel companies. While they contribute, she observes conflicting information from:
- Right-wing media (more prevalent)
- Left-wing media (less but still present)  
- Climate advocates themselves (inadvertently)

**Reasoning:** "This challenge would actually just be easier if you could just point and say, 'Well, it's the fossil fuel companies are obviously doing this.' But you do also just see this kind of conflicting information coming from other [sources]."

**Implication:** The information environment is more complex than a simple good-vs-evil narrative, requiring more sophisticated communication strategies.

### Nuclear's Limited Role

**Contrarian View:** While not dismissing nuclear entirely, Ritchie argues it's **too expensive for bulk electricity generation** and should be considered primarily for the expensive final percentage points of decarbonization—contrary to nuclear advocates who see it as a primary solution.

**Reasoning:** Even in countries building nuclear relatively cheaply (China, South Korea), it remains more expensive than renewables. The rapid cost decline and deployment speed of solar and wind make them the obvious choice for the bulk of decarbonization.

**Host's Addition:** Liebreich goes further, questioning whether Asian nuclear is actually as cheap as portrayed once hidden subsidies are accounted for, and challenging nuclear's suitability even as backup power due to inflexibility.

### The EV Battery Degradation Non-Problem

**Contrarian View:** Against widespread concern about EV battery degradation, Ritchie presents data showing **batteries retain 85%+ capacity after 200,000 miles**—far better than consumer electronics that shape public perception.

**Reasoning:** The industry dynamics differ fundamentally from phones. Car manufacturers know their market depends on battery longevity, creating strong incentives for durability that don't exist in consumer electronics.

### Mineral Dependency Is Manageable

**Contrarian View:** While acknowledging risks, Ritchie argues that **mineral dependency for clean energy is fundamentally different and less threatening than fossil fuel dependency**, contrary to growing alarm about Chinese dominance of supply chains.

**Reasoning:** Once you own the equipment, you're energy-independent. Fossil fuels require continuous imports to function at all.

**Host's Counterpoint:** Liebreich offers a contrarian take on the contrarian take, arguing this underestimates supply chain vulnerabilities for maintenance, replacement, and expansion—plus security risks from data collection capabilities in imported equipment.

## 5. Practical Implications

### For Individual Decision-Making

**EV Purchases:** Consumers can confidently purchase EVs knowing:
- Climate benefits are real and substantial, even on today's grids
- Battery degradation concerns are overblown
- Cold weather range reduction (~15%) is manageable for most use cases
- The technology will only improve as grids clean up

**Transparency Matters:** Manufacturers should clearly communicate limitations (like cold weather range) rather than overpromising. Informed consumers make better decisions and have more realistic expectations.

### For Policy and Investment

**Grid Infrastructure Priority:** The conversation reveals that **grid connection and permitting reform may be more urgent than solving intermittency**. Policymakers should prioritize:
- Merit-based connection queues
- Permitting process modernization
- Transmission infrastructure investment

**The 95% Strategy:** Governments should focus on rapidly achieving 90-95% clean electricity rather than obsessing over 100% perfection. The resources saved can decarbonize other sectors more cost-effectively.

**Supply Chain Diversification:** While proceeding with deployment, countries should simultaneously:
- Develop domestic manufacturing capacity
- Diversify supplier relationships
- Accept that this involves cost and time trade-offs
- Consider partnerships with emerging economies (India, Southeast Asia, Africa)

### For Understanding Macro Trends

**Developed vs. Developing World Dynamics:** The conversation highlights how **developing countries may transition faster** because:
- They're accustomed to electricity demand growth
- Their grids are younger and more adaptable
- They lack legacy infrastructure and institutional inertia

**The China Factor:** Understanding the US-China rivalry as competing dependencies (US LNG vs. Chinese clean tech) helps explain policy tensions. European countries face the challenge of avoiding dependency on either power.

**Cost Trajectories:** The Nissan Leaf example (4x range increase, 2x power increase, 3x faster charging, same nominal cost over 15 years) illustrates that **clean technology cost curves continue improving rapidly**. Any analysis becomes conservative quickly.

### For Market Participants

**Timing Matters:** The book's content becomes "pessimistic or conservative" quickly as technology advances. Market participants should assume:
- Faster cost declines than historical data suggests
- Quicker deployment than official projections
- Continued surprises on the upside for clean tech

**The Middle Market:** The "confused middle" represents the largest market opportunity. Products and communications that provide clarity and address specific concerns (like cold weather EV performance) can capture this segment.

**Sectoral Priorities:** With electricity approaching 90-95% feasibility, attention and investment should shift toward:
- Transportation electrification
- Heating decarbonization  
- Industrial processes
- The "final 5%" solutions (long-duration storage, hydrogen, etc.)

### Actionable Takeaways

1. **For Advocates:** Use Ritchie's question-based framework rather than attacking misinformation directly. Equip people with context to evaluate claims themselves.

2. **For Skeptics:** Recognize that most concerns about clean energy have solid, data-driven answers. The question is whether you're willing to engage with the evidence.

3. **For Policymakers:** Stop relitigating solved questions (like whether EVs work) and focus on implementation barriers (like grid connections and permitting).

4. **For Investors:** The transition is happening faster than official projections. Position for continued cost declines and deployment acceleration in solar, wind, batteries, and EVs.

5. **For Consumers:** Make decisions based on current technology and economics, knowing that the climate benefits are real and the technology will only improve.

6. **For Communicators:** Target the "confused middle" rather than trying to convert the strongly opposed. Provide clear, accessible information that helps people make sense of conflicting claims.
